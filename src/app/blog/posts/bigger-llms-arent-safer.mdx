The v0.5 paper ran on the OpenAI lineup: GPT-5, GPT-5-mini, GPT-5-nano, GPT-4.1. The headline within-OpenAI result was already counterintuitive: the older, non-reasoning GPT-4.1 was the safest model at default settings. The hypothesis we offered was a model-class effect — with default "minimal" reasoning, the GPT-5 family elaborates more freely, produces longer plans, and longer plans introduce more blacklisted content.

That hypothesis predicts something testable. If the pattern is *capability* and not *vendor*, then the cross-vendor extension to Anthropic should show the same shape: the flagship in the Claude lineup should be the worst raw-safety performer, and the cheapest should be among the safest.

In v0.6, I ran it. Here's the data.

Two things to read off this table that the OpenAI-only data couldn't have told you:

1. **The flagship pattern reproduces across vendors.** Anthropic's flagship (Opus 4.7) is the least-safe model in the lineup. OpenAI's flagship (GPT-5) is the second-least-safe. The two cheapest models (Haiku, GPT-4.1) are tied for safest. This is not a vendor problem. It's a tier problem.

2. **Cross-vendor at-tier is approximately commodity for safety.** Haiku ≈ GPT-4.1 ≈ GPT-5-nano. Sonnet ≈ GPT-5-mini. The within-tier vendor gaps are smaller than the across-tier gaps in the same vendor. If you pick a tier, you can largely pick a vendor on other criteria.

## The mechanism, sharpened

Here's the hypothesis from the OpenAI-only paper:

> Default minimal reasoning makes the GPT-5 family elaborate more freely, producing longer plans. Longer plans introduce more blacklisted content. Per-prescription error rate may be similar; absolute violation count rises with verbosity.

The cross-vendor data lets me sharpen this. The pattern isn't reasoning-mode specific. It isn't vendor specific. It's capability specific. More capable models — whatever the vendor, whatever the reasoning configuration — generate longer, more elaborate, more confident outputs. The Lane A scorer counts violations as absolute occurrences; more output is more violations.

This is consistent with the reasoning-effort sensitivity result the paper documents for GPT-5: the flagship benefits *monotonically* from more reasoning effort, while the mid-tier and cheap models get *less* safe with more reasoning. The flagship can think its way out of a contraindicated prescription; the small models, given more thinking budget, just produce more confidently broken plans.

In the v0.6 Anthropic data, the picture is consistent. Opus 4.7's outputs are longer than Haiku's. Opus has a new tokenizer (~35% more tokens versus Opus 4.1, vendor-disclosed) and rejects the temperature parameter entirely. Even controlling for those mechanical differences as best I can, Opus writes more prescriptions per prompt, more confidently, with a higher absolute count of contraindicated ones.

The label "Opus is unsafe" is editorial. The measurable claim is: **Opus's raw output without governance has more violations than smaller models on the same scenarios.** That is what the data says. Whether you call that "unsafe" is a values question downstream of the measurement.

## What this means for "AI safety" as a marketing claim

There's a thing AI vendors do — all of them — where the larger models in a lineup get described as both more capable *and* safer. Better reasoning, better alignment, better refusal behavior, better edge-case handling. The implicit framing is that capability and safety move together: the more capable model is also the more responsible one.

The v0.6 cross-vendor data shows this is, at least for one specific deployment pattern, exactly backwards. The most capable models in two vendors' lineups produced the *most* violations of clinical contraindications when deployed in the way consumer AI products actually deploy them: raw prose, free-form prompt, no structural validation downstream.

I don't think this is because the labs are lying. I think the safety evaluations the labs run — the ones that make it into model cards — are measuring different failure modes. Refusal behavior on overtly dangerous prompts. Bias on standardized benchmarks. Hallucination on factual questions. Those are *real* failure modes and worth measuring. But they are not the failure modes a wrapped-LLM consumer product is most exposed to. The mode that's most exposed is the one where the model confidently generates a plausible-looking plan that has a contraindicated exercise in it because the model wasn't paying attention to a constraint the user mentioned once.

That's not a "the lab's safety training failed" problem. That's a "the lab's safety training is for a different distribution than the production distribution" problem. The model is doing what it was trained to do. It's producing useful, fluent, expert-sounding output. The mismatch between that and "ship to a real human with a real meniscus" is not a model failure. It's an architectural failure of how the model is deployed.

## What this means for your buying decision

I want to be operationally specific. If you're picking an LLM for a clinical or wellness AI product in 2026, the cross-vendor data suggests three things:

1. **Don't pay flagship prices for safety.** Flagship pricing buys you fluency, reasoning, instruction-following, longer context. It does not buy you fewer contraindicated prescriptions on its own. If the safety story is what you're paying for, you're overpaying.

2. **Pick a tier on cost, capability, or latency — and put the safety mechanism elsewhere.** Within a tier, vendor choice matters less than you'd expect. Pick the tier whose unit economics work for your product, then add the governance layer that does the actual safety work.

3. **The cheap tier with governance beats the expensive tier without.** Haiku + WPL across 180 trials: zero safety violations. Opus alone across 30 trials: 114 violations. The cost ratio between those two configurations is roughly 27 to 1, in the cheap tier's favor. There is no reading of the data where picking Opus without a contract is preferable to picking Haiku with one, unless you specifically need Opus's other capabilities for unrelated reasons.

## What this doesn't mean

I don't want this to be heard as "Anthropic is bad" or "OpenAI is bad" or "the labs are reckless." None of that is the takeaway.

The takeaway is: **the labs are optimizing for one set of objectives; product teams are deploying for a different set; and the gap between those two sets of objectives is exactly the place a governance layer has to live.** The labs aren't going to close this gap, because the gap is downstream of what a lab is structurally capable of doing. They can't know your client's contraindications. They can't apply your blacklist. They can't enforce per-client rules on every regeneration. Those are product-team responsibilities.

The data also doesn't mean flagships should never be used. The reasoning-effort sensitivity result is real: GPT-5 at high reasoning is safer than GPT-5 at minimal, and probably safer than GPT-4.1 at any setting. If your product can afford the reasoning budget and your unit economics tolerate it, the flagship is fine — *with* governance. Without governance, the flagship without reasoning is the worst-of-both-worlds combination.

## The cleaner framing

The clickbait version is "bigger LLMs aren't safer." That's true for the specific operational claim "more raw safety violations on this benchmark." It is not true in any general sense.

The cleaner framing is: **safety is not a property of the model. Safety is a property of the system that includes the model.** The data the cross-vendor sweep produced doesn't tell you which model is safest; it tells you that the question is poorly formed. The model is one input to the safety property, and not the dominant one — at least not for the deployment pattern most consumer AI products actually use.
