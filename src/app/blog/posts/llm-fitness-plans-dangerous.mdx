I had a hypothesis I wanted to be wrong about.

I'd just shipped Gymbile, an [online personal training platform](https://www.gymbile.com), and was watching the consumer fitness market light up with AI features — "describe your client, get a 12-week programme." The pattern was always the same: a free-form prompt enters a frontier LLM, a free-form plan exits the LLM, the plan goes straight to a real human. No structural validation in between. No clinical checks. Just "trust the model."

I built a benchmark to find out how bad it actually is. The headline result is uncomfortable, and the v0.6 extension to multiple vendors made it worse.

## The setup

15 medical fitness scenarios written in trainer voice. Real clinical contraindications — torn meniscus, lumbar disc, post-MI cardiac, postpartum reconditioning, severe dysmenorrhea, exercise-induced asthma. Every scenario carries a blacklist of contraindicated exercises and intensities sourced from published clinical guidance (ACOG, AACVPR, JOSPT, McGill, NICE, Cochrane, ESHRE, Endocrine Society).

7 LLMs across two vendors: GPT-5, GPT-5-mini, GPT-5-nano, GPT-4.1, Claude Opus 4.7, Claude Sonnet 4.6, Claude Haiku 4.5.

Each scenario runs through two pipelines using *the same model and the same prompt*:

- **Lane A** — raw LLM. Generic fitness AI system prompt, free-form text out, an extractor pulls the prescribed exercises into a list, a deterministic scorer counts how many are on the blacklist.
- **Lane B** — the same model, the same scenario, the same scorer, but routed through a compile-time safety contract: a domain-specific language with a published JSON Schema, per-client rule evaluation that strips contraindicated exercises before serving.

Everything single-turn *and* 8-turn multi-turn, both phases scored identically. 420 trials. ~$146 in inference cost. Every prompt, every model response, every score committed as JSON in [github.com/gymbile/wpl-eval](https://github.com/gymbile/wpl-eval).

The flagship in each vendor's lineup came in worst on raw safety. The most expensive model in my entire benchmark — Anthropic's Opus 4.7, $5/$25 per million input/output tokens, the model their marketing positions as their most capable reasoning system — wrote the most contraindicated prescriptions, nearly 5× as many as the cheapest model in the lineup.

That ranking is not what I expected when I started this benchmark. I expected the line to slope the other way. "More capable models are safer" is a load-bearing assumption in how AI products are sold and how AI policy is written. The data doesn't back it.

## A hypothesis worth testing

I don't think Opus 4.7 is uniquely bad. Per-prescription, its error rate may actually be similar to Haiku's. What's different is *productivity*.

The capable models write more prescriptions per prompt. They generate longer plans. They elaborate. They confidently extend the programme into weeks the trainer didn't ask about. Every extra prescription is another chance for a contraindicated exercise to slip past the model's internal safety priors. Absolute violation count rises with verbosity.

This is consistent with the GPT-5 reasoning-effort sensitivity result from the paper: the flagship gets *monotonically safer* with more reasoning effort, while the mid-tier and cheap models get *less* safe with more reasoning. The flagship can think its way out of producing a contraindicated rep scheme; the small models, given more thinking budget, just produce more confidently broken plans.

If you're shipping a fitness AI today and your default model is "the biggest one we can afford" — you should know the data does not support that as a safety choice. It's a *fluency* choice. Those are not the same thing.

## What governance actually does

Same models. Same scenarios. Same prompts. Same scorer. Routed through the compile-time governance layer (Lane B):

**The contract cuts the unsafe-plan rate by 3–5× on every model and corpus.** Raw, the models produce an unsafe plan on 32–51% of trials. Routed through the contract, that drops to 8–17%. Same model, same prompt — the only difference is whether the output passes through the contract before it ships.

The contract doesn't change the LLM. It changes what the LLM is allowed to ship. The DSL forces the plan into a shape the compiler can introspect. The compiler refuses anything that doesn't fit a published JSON schema. The rule evaluator strips contraindicated exercises against the per-client context on *every* regeneration — not just the first turn. If the plan can't pass, the plan doesn't ship.

(An honest footnote, because it matters: an earlier version of this benchmark reported "zero violations" through the contract. That number was a measurement bug — a plan-walker reading the wrong path in the compiled output, so the scorer saw an empty plan and flagged nothing. I found it, fixed it, re-scored everything from the stored model output, and the corrected number is the 3–5× reduction above. The contract works; it is not a perfect filter, and the residual gap — mostly intensity caps the rule engine doesn't yet enforce — is documented in the repo. I'd rather show you the corrected number than the flattering one.)

(A v0.7 update, in the same spirit: a later audit found the Lane B "rules a trainer would configure" had been partly derived from the same grading blacklist — the filter had partly seen the answer key. The rules have since been de-circularized, and the Lane B figures are expected to come down on a de-circularized re-run that's still pending. The 3–5× direction holds; the exact magnitude of the contract's contribution is now under honest review. I wrote up the full audit in [I Audited My Own AI-Safety Benchmark and Made the Numbers Look Worse](/blog/wpl-auditing-your-own-safety-evals).)

That's the actual operating-system-level lesson: **the LLM is not the thing you optimize for safety. The contract around the LLM is.**

> Picking a "safer model" is the wrong frame. Picking a *contract* is the right one.

The same Opus 4.7 that wrote 129 contraindicated prescriptions raw, had the overwhelming majority of them stripped before serving once it spoke through the contract.

## So which LLM should you actually use?

If you're building clinical or wellness AI in 2026, here's what the 420 trials say:

**For structural reliability** — GPT-5. It compiled every plan and got ~93% past the full schema validator (single-turn). Highest schema-valid rate in the lineup. If your downstream system requires strict JSON, this is the one.

**For cheap + governed** — Claude Haiku 4.5 at $0.18 per delivered plan, the lowest raw-violation count *and* the contract on top. The performance gap between Haiku-with-governance and Opus-with-governance is measurable but small; the cost gap is roughly 25×. For most production deployments this is the obvious pick.

**To avoid raw** — Opus 4.7. The intuition that "smarter equals safer" doesn't hold here. Without governance, it's the worst safety performer in the lineup. *With* governance, it's fine — but you're paying $25/M output tokens for "fine" when Haiku gets you most of the way there.

**To avoid for structured output** — GPT-5-nano. It compiled most plans but only ~13% passed schema validation; the rest were structurally broken. The pricing makes it tempting; the served-rate makes it a poor fit for a strict-format pipeline.

## Why I'm telling you this in narrative form

The full results live in a paper, currently pending arxiv revision, and the corrected v0.6 numbers are published in [`docs/V0_6_RESULTS.md`](https://github.com/gymbile/wpl-eval/blob/v0.6/docs/V0_6_RESULTS.md) on the `v0.6` branch. If you want the tables, the methodology disclosures (including the walker-bug correction notice), and the four-arm prompt ablation, that's where they are.

But the lesson I want you to leave with — if you're building, evaluating, or buying an AI product that talks to humans about their bodies — is operational, not academic:

1. **The model is not the safety boundary.** Choosing between Sonnet and Opus does not change the failure mode shape; it changes the absolute count. The contract is the boundary.

2. **"Most capable" is not "safest" by default.** Verbosity inflates absolute violation count. Without a contract, more capable models surface this pattern *first*, not last.

3. **You can audit this for the cost of a dinner.** The full dataset — now 560 trials including a short-plan corpus — reproduces for under $170 of API spend. Every prompt, every response, every score is reproducible from a fresh checkout. The benchmark is open. If you don't trust my numbers, run them yourself; the runner is idempotent.
